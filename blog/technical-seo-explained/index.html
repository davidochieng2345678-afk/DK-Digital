1. Crawling vs Indexing – What's the Difference?

Crawling is the process where Googlebot (Google's crawler) follows links from known pages to discover new pages. It requests your pages, reads the HTML, and follows any links it finds. Indexing is when Google processes the crawled page, analyzes its content, and stores it in its database (the index). Only indexed pages can appear in search results.

Think of crawling as “visiting” your page, and indexing as “filing it away” for future searches. A page can be crawled but not indexed (e.g., if it has a noindex tag, or if Google deems it low quality). Conversely, a page cannot be indexed without being crawled first.

Common failure points:

  • Google never crawls the page (no links to it, or robots.txt blocks it).
  • Google crawls the page but does not index it (noindex tag, duplicate content, poor quality).
  • Google indexes the page but later drops it (soft 404, redirects, or page becomes low quality).

Action: Use Google Search Console → “Pages” report. It shows “Indexed” vs “Not indexed” pages. If you have important pages in “Not indexed”, fix the issues. Complete GSC indexing guide →

2. How to Check if Google Is Crawling Your Site (Crawl Stats)

Google Search Console provides detailed crawl statistics. Go to “Settings” → “Crawl stats”. Key metrics:

  • Total crawl requests: How many times Googlebot requested pages on your site per day.
  • Pages crawled per day: Trend line – should be stable or increasing as you add content.
  • Time spent downloading a page (ms): High values indicate slow server response.
  • Crawl errors (404s, 5xx): High error rates waste crawl budget.

Warning signs:

  • Sudden drop in crawl requests → Google may have lost trust or your server became slow.
  • High percentage of 404 errors → many broken links.
  • Low pages crawled per day relative to your total pages → crawl budget issue.

If you see crawl stats declining, investigate server uptime, robots.txt, and broken links. Understanding crawl stats →

3. Robots.txt – The Gatekeeper of Crawling

The robots.txt file tells search engine crawlers which parts of your site to not crawl. It lives at https://yourdomain.com/robots.txt. A misconfigured robots.txt is one of the most common indexing disasters.

3.1 Common Robots.txt Mistakes

  • Disallow: / – This blocks all crawlers from your entire site. Your site will vanish from Google.
  • Disallow: /wp-admin/ (correct) – But accidentally Disallow: /wp- would block /wp-content/ (themes, images).
  • Missing sitemap directive – Adding Sitemap: https://yoursite.com/sitemap.xml helps Google find your sitemap.
  • Blocking CSS/JS files – Google needs these to render your page properly for mobile‑first indexing.

3.2 How to Test Your Robots.txt

In Google Search Console → “URL inspection” tool → “Test live URL” → “Robots.txt tester”. It shows which directives apply to the URL. Also use Robots.txt Tester in GSC (left menu) to validate syntax.

3.3 Sample Robots.txt (Recommended)

User-agent: *
Allow: /
Disallow: /admin/
Disallow: /wp-admin/
Sitemap: https://yoursite.com/sitemap.xml

Action: Visit your /robots.txt. Ensure it does not block important pages. Remove any Disallow: / line. Complete robots.txt guide →

4. Meta Robots Tags – Page‑Level Indexing Control

Meta robots tags give you page‑by‑page control over crawling and indexing. They go in the `` section of your HTML.

4.1 Common Meta Robots Directives

  • <meta name="robots" content="index, follow"> – Allow indexing and following links (default).
  • <meta name="robots" content="noindex"> – Do not index this page (but still follow links).
  • <meta name="robots" content="nofollow"> – Do not follow links on this page (but index the page).
  • <meta name="robots" content="noindex, nofollow"> – Neither index nor follow links.

4.2 Common Meta Robots Mistakes

  • Accidental noindex on important pages – e.g., staging site noindex copied to production.
  • Using noindex on paginated pages (e.g., /page/2) – Google may still need to crawl them to discover deeper content. Use rel=prev/next instead.
  • Noindex on thank‑you pages – This is fine (users don't need to search for thank‑you pages).

4.3 How to Check Meta Robots Tags

View page source (Ctrl+U) and search for “robots”. Or use the “URL inspection” tool in GSC – it shows the meta robots directive Google sees.

Action: Ensure your important pages (homepage, services, product pages) have index, follow. Remove any accidental noindex tags. Meta robots guide →

5. XML Sitemaps – Your Roadmap for Google

An XML sitemap is a file that lists all the pages you want Google to know about. It is not mandatory, but it helps Google discover pages that might not have many internal links.

5.1 Why You Need a Sitemap

  • Helps Google find orphan pages (pages with no internal links).
  • Provides metadata: last modified date, priority, change frequency.
  • Speeds up indexing of new content.

5.2 How to Create a Sitemap (Free)

  • WordPress: Use Yoast SEO or Rank Math – they generate sitemaps automatically at /sitemap_index.xml.
  • Static HTML sites: Use a free online generator like XML‑Sitemaps.com (crawl up to 500 pages free). Download the sitemap.xml file and upload to your root directory.
  • Screaming Frog: Crawl your site → Sitemap → Create XML sitemap.

5.3 How to Submit Your Sitemap to Google

In Google Search Console → “Sitemaps” → Enter sitemap URL (e.g., sitemap.xml) → Submit. Check the status – it should say “Success”.

5.4 Common Sitemap Mistakes

  • Including noindex pages – Remove them from the sitemap; it confuses Google.
  • Not updating sitemap after adding/removing pages – Regenerate your sitemap whenever you publish new content.
  • Sitemap too large (over 50,000 URLs) – Split into multiple sitemap files.

Action: Generate a sitemap, upload it to your server, and submit to GSC. Check that all important pages are included. Complete XML sitemap guide →

6. Orphan Pages – Pages With No Internal Links

Orphan pages are pages that no other page on your site links to. Google may never discover them (unless they are in your sitemap). Even if discovered, they receive zero link equity and rank poorly.

6.1 How to Find Orphan Pages

Use Screaming Frog (free up to 500 URLs). Crawl your site. Go to “Internal” tab → “Inlinks” column. Pages with 0 inlinks (excluding the homepage) are orphans.

6.2 How to Fix Orphan Pages

  • Add links from relevant parent pages. If the orphan is a product, link from its category page. If it's a blog post, link from a newer, related post.
  • Add links from your “Related Posts” section. Manually curate related content.
  • Update older content to link to the orphan page. Find 3-5 existing pages that are topically relevant and add a link.
  • If the page is truly unnecessary, delete it or merge it with another page. Then add a 301 redirect to the surviving page.

Action: Run Screaming Frog monthly. Fix orphan pages by adding internal links. Orphan pages fix guide →

7. Crawl Budget – Don't Waste Google's Time

Crawl budget is the number of pages Googlebot will crawl on your site within a given time. If you have a large site (10,000+ pages) or a site with many low‑value pages, you need to manage crawl budget.

7.1 Factors That Waste Crawl Budget

  • 404 errors: Googlebot visits dead pages repeatedly.
  • Redirect chains: A → B → C → D. Each redirect adds time.
  • Low‑value pages: Tag pages, archive pages, parameter URLs (sorting/filtering).
  • Duplicate content: Google crawls the same content multiple times.
  • Slow server response (TTFB): Googlebot spends more time waiting, so fewer pages are crawled.

7.2 How to Optimize Crawl Budget

  • Fix 404 errors (see Reason #5 in ranking guide or technical area #6).
  • Use 301 redirects instead of 302 for permanent moves, and avoid chains.
  • Block low‑value pages in robots.txt or add noindex tags.
  • Use canonical tags to consolidate duplicate content.
  • Improve server speed (TTFB <200ms).
  • Submit an accurate XML sitemap to guide Googlebot to your most important pages.

For most Kenyan small business sites (under 1,000 pages), crawl budget is rarely an issue. But for large e‑commerce or directory sites, optimization is critical. Crawl budget optimization guide →

9. Common Crawling & Indexing Mistakes (Even After Fixing)

  • ❌ Using “noindex” on pages that have backlinks. You lose link equity. Remove noindex and use 301 redirect if the page should be gone.
  • ❌ Blocking CSS/JS in robots.txt. Google needs these to render mobile pages. Do not block them.
  • ❌ Not updating sitemap after site changes. Regenerate sitemap weekly and resubmit to GSC.
  • ❌ Relying only on sitemap without internal links. Google prefers following links. Sitemaps are backups, not primary discovery method.
  • ❌ Ignoring the “Page indexing” report in GSC. It tells you exactly which pages are not indexed and why (crawl anomaly, blocked by robots, 404, etc.). Check it weekly.

3. Common Speed Mistakes (Kenyan Websites)

From our audit database, these are the most frequent speed errors:

  • Mistake 5A: Unoptimized images. Using raw JPEGs from a camera (2-5MB each) instead of compressed WebP (100-200KB). Images account for 70% of page weight on most Kenyan sites.
  • Mistake 5B: No caching. Every visitor downloads the same CSS, JS, and images from scratch. Browser caching can reduce repeat load time by 80%.
  • Mistake 5C: Poor hosting (international server, shared). A US-based shared host adds 300-600ms latency to every request for Kenyan users.
  • Mistake 5D: Render‑blocking JavaScript and CSS. The browser loads these before showing anything, delaying first paint by seconds.
  • Mistake 5E: No CDN. Content Delivery Networks cache static assets on edge servers closer to users. Without a CDN, each request travels to the origin server.
  • Mistake 5F: Too many external scripts (ads, trackers, chat widgets). Each script adds network requests and JavaScript execution time.
  • Mistake 5G: Not using modern image formats. JPEG/PNG are larger than WebP or AVIF.

Fix these in order: Images → Hosting/CDN → Caching → Critical CSS → Remove render‑blocking resources.

5. Step‑by‑Step Speed Fixes (From Easy to Advanced)

Fix #1: Compress and Convert Images (1 hour – huge impact)

Images are the #1 speed killer. Use these free tools:

  • Squoosh.app: Drag and drop images, convert to WebP, reduce quality to 80-85%. No visible difference, file size drops 50-70%.
  • TinyPNG / TinyJPG: Batch compress up to 20 images at once.
  • For bulk conversion (advanced): Use ImageMagick command line or a plugin.

Target file sizes: Hero image under 200KB, product images under 100KB, icons under 20KB.

Serve WebP/AVIF: Use `` element with fallback:

<picture>
  <source srcset="image.avif" type="image/avif">
  <source srcset="image.webp" type="image/webp">
  <img src="image.jpg" alt="description" loading="lazy">
</picture>

Fix #2: Enable Browser Caching (15 minutes)

Add to your `.htaccess` file (Apache) or nginx config:

<IfModule mod_expires.c>
  ExpiresActive On
  ExpiresByType image/jpeg "access plus 1 year"
  ExpiresByType image/webp "access plus 1 year"
  ExpiresByType text/css "access plus 1 month"
  ExpiresByType application/javascript "access plus 1 month"
</IfModule>

For WordPress, use a caching plugin like LiteSpeed Cache or W3 Total Cache.

Fix #3: Use a CDN (30 minutes – free)

A CDN stores copies of your site on servers around the world. For Kenyan users, a CDN with an African edge server dramatically reduces latency.

Cloudflare (free plan): Sign up, change your domain’s nameservers to Cloudflare’s. It includes a Nairobi edge location. TTFB can drop from 400ms to 50ms for cached assets.

Other CDNs: BunnyCDN (Johannesburg edge), or use your host’s built‑in CDN.

Fix #4: Choose Kenya‑Friendly Hosting (Critical)

If your host’s servers are in the US or Europe, every request has a 200-500ms round trip before any content is sent. Options for better TTFB from Kenya:

  • Truehost Kenya: Local servers in Nairobi. TTFB 50-150ms. Affordable.
  • HostPoa: Kenyan hosting, good support.
  • Cloudflare CDN (free): Keep your existing host but route through Cloudflare – cached assets served from Nairobi edge.
  • Netlify / Vercel (static sites): Their global CDN includes African edges. Excellent for static HTML sites.
  • AWS Africa (Cape Town): Best for dynamic sites needing full control. Latency ~150ms from Nairobi.

Fix #5: Enable Brotli Compression (Better than Gzip)

Brotli compresses text (HTML, CSS, JS) 20-30% smaller than Gzip. Most modern hosts support it. Test at https://www.brotli-tester.com/. If not enabled, ask your host to enable.

Fix #6: Defer Render‑Blocking JavaScript

PageSpeed Insights will list “Eliminate render‑blocking resources”. For scripts not needed for above‑the‑fold content, add `defer` or `async`:

<script src="non-critical.js" defer></script>
<script src="analytics.js" async></script>
