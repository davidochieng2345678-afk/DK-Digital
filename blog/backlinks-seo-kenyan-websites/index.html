1. Do‑Follow vs No‑Follow Backlinks – What’s the Difference?

Understanding link attributes is essential before you start building backlinks.

  • Do‑follow links: The default link type. They pass “link equity” (PageRank) from the source page to your site. These directly improve your rankings. Most backlinks you build should be do‑follow.
  • No‑follow links: Links with `rel="nofollow"` attribute. They tell Google not to pass link equity. No‑follow links do not directly improve rankings, but they still bring traffic, brand visibility, and can look natural in your link profile. It’s healthy to have a mix (10‑20% no‑follow).
  • Sponsored links: `rel="sponsored"` – for paid links. Use this if you pay for a link (e.g., sponsored guest post).
  • UGC links: `rel="ugc"` – for user‑generated content (forums, comments).

Actionable insight: When building backlinks, prioritise do‑follow links from relevant, high‑authority sites. But don’t reject no‑follow links – they are natural and help your link profile look organic.

3. Toxic Backlinks – What to Avoid at All Costs

These types of backlinks can trigger Google penalties. Avoid them completely.

  • Paid links (undisclosed): If you pay for a link and it is not marked `rel="sponsored"`, it violates Google’s guidelines. Google detects paid links through algorithms and manual reviews.
  • Link farms / PBNs (Private Blog Networks): Networks of low‑quality sites created solely to pass link equity. Google actively deindexes these.
  • Spammy directories: Directories that accept any submission with no editorial review (often free). These have no authority and can be toxic.
  • Comment spam: Links in blog comments, especially with keyword‑rich anchor text. Most comment links are no‑follow, but excessive comment spam can still trigger penalties.
  • Forum signatures: Links in forum signatures (e.g., old-style “posted by user with signature link”). Many are low quality.
  • Article directories: Sites like EzineArticles, ArticleCube – these are obsolete and often considered spam.
  • Widget links: Links embedded in “free widgets” (e.g., “Powered by...”). Google devalues these.
  • Links from hacked sites: If a spammer injects links to your site on a hacked website, you need to disavow them.

⚠️ Warning Signs of a Toxic Backlink Source:

  • The site has very low DA (<10) and looks spammy (poor design, no real content).
  • The site accepts any submission without review (automated approval).
  • The site has many outbound links (500+ per page).
  • The site is not indexed by Google (search `site:example.com` – if no results, avoid).
  • The site is in a language unrelated to your business (e.g., Russian or Chinese sites linking to your Kenyan business).

4. How to Audit Your Existing Backlinks for Toxicity (Free Tools)

Before you build new backlinks, audit your existing profile. You may already have toxic links that are hurting your rankings.

4.1 Using Google Search Console (Free)

GSC → “Links” → “External links” → “Top linking sites”. This lists the domains linking to you. Click on each domain and visit the site. If it looks spammy, note it down. GSC does not flag toxicity, but you can manually review.

4.2 Using Ahrefs Webmaster Tools (Free)

Ahrefs has a “Backlink profile” report with a “Toxic score” (0-100). Scroll down – look for links with a red “toxic” flag. Click on the backlink to see why it is flagged (e.g., “Site has low traffic”, “Site has many outbound links”). This is the easiest free method.

4.3 Using Moz Link Explorer (Free limited)

Enter your domain → “Inbound links” → “Spam score” (0-17). Links with spam score 5+ may be toxic. Investigate.

4.4 Manual Review (Most thorough)

Export your backlinks from Ahrefs or GSC. Open the list in Google Sheets. For each domain, visit the site. Ask: “Does this site look legitimate? Is it relevant to my business? Does it have real content and traffic?” If no, mark it as toxic.

5. What Is a Google Manual Action? (And How to Check)

A manual action is when a Google human reviewer determines that your site violates Google’s Webmaster Guidelines. Common reasons: unnatural links (buying links, link schemes), thin content, or spammy structured data.

How to check if you have a manual action:

  • Log into Google Search Console → “Security & Manual Actions” → “Manual actions”.
  • If it says “No issues detected”, you are safe. If it says “Unnatural links to your site” or similar, you have a penalty.

What a manual action means for your rankings: Your pages may be de‑indexed or severely demoted. You will see a sharp traffic drop. You must fix the issue and submit a reconsideration request to Google.

1. What Is Broken Link Building – And Why It Works

Broken link building is a three‑step process:

  1. Find a broken link (404 error) on a relevant website.
  2. Create (or find) a piece of content on your site that matches the broken link's topic.
  3. Contact the website owner, inform them of the broken link, and suggest your content as a replacement.

Why it works: Website owners want their sites to be functional. Broken links frustrate users and hurt SEO. If you provide a helpful replacement, they are likely to accept your suggestion. You get a quality backlink; they fix a broken link. Win‑win.

📊 Success Rate: With a well‑executed broken link building campaign, you can expect a 10‑20% conversion rate (every 10 emails yields 1-2 new backlinks). This is higher than cold guest post pitching (which is often 5‑10%).

1. When to Use the Disavow Tool (And When NOT To)

Use the disavow tool if:

  • You have a manual action in Google Search Console under “Security & Manual Actions” saying “Unnatural links to your site”.
  • You have many toxic backlinks (spammy domains, low DR, irrelevant language, adult or gambling sites).
  • You previously bought backlinks from Fiverr, Upwork, or other cheap services.
  • You have been the target of a negative SEO attack (someone built spammy links to your site).

Do NOT use the disavow tool if:

  • You are not sure if a link is toxic.
  • You have no manual action and your backlink profile is generally clean.
  • You have not first tried to remove the links manually (contact site owners).

Disavowing good links can hurt your rankings. Only disavow links that you are certain are toxic.

2. How to Identify Toxic Backlinks (Free Methods)

2.1 Using Ahrefs Webmaster Tools (Recommended)

  1. Log into Ahrefs Webmaster Tools.
  2. Go to “Backlinks” report.
  3. Look for the “Toxic score” column (red icon indicates toxic).
  4. Click through to see why Ahrefs flagged it (e.g., “Site has low traffic”, “Site has many outbound links”, “Site is not indexed”, “Site is in a foreign language”).
  5. Export the list of toxic domains.

2.2 Manual Review of Suspect Domains

For each suspect domain, visit the site. Ask these questions:

  • Does the site have real content (not spun or auto‑generated)?
  • Is the site indexed by Google? (Search `site:domain.com` – if no results, it is not indexed.)
  • Does the site have many outbound links (hundreds per page)?
  • Is the site in a language irrelevant to your business (e.g., Russian, Chinese, Arabic)?
  • Does the site look spammy (pop‑ups, ads, no design)?
  • Did you pay for this link? (If yes, disavow it.)

If you answer “yes” to 2+ of these, disavow the entire domain.

2.3 Using Google Search Console (Limited)

GSC does not flag toxic links, but you can manually review “Top linking sites”. If you see domains you do not recognise, investigate them.

3. How to Create a Disavow File (Correct Format)

A disavow file is a simple .txt file. Each line tells Google to ignore a specific link or entire domain.

3.1 Format for Disavowing an Entire Domain

domain:spamsite.com

This tells Google to ignore all links from any page on that domain. This is the most common format.

3.2 Format for Disavowing a Specific URL

https://spamsite.com/spam-page.html

Use this only if you want to keep links from other pages on the same domain.

3.3 Comments in Disavow File

You can add comments (ignored by Google) by starting a line with #. Use this to document why you disavowed a domain.

3.4 Example Disavow File

# Disavow file for yourbusiness.com
# Created June 13, 2026
# Toxic link farms
domain:lowqualitydirectory1.com
domain:linkfarm2.net
# Spammy foreign sites
domain:spam-russian-site.ru
# Specific paid link page
https://cheap-backlinks-store.com/buy-links.html

3.5 How to Create the File

  1. Open Notepad (Windows) or TextEdit (Mac) in plain text mode.
  2. List each domain (one per line) in the format above.
  3. Save as `disavow.txt`.

4. How to Submit the Disavow File via Google Search Console

  1. Log into Google Search Console.
  2. Select your website property.
  3. In the left menu, find “Legacy tools and reports” → “Disavow links”. (If you don’t see it, search for “Google Disavow Tool”.)
  4. Select your property again.
  5. Click “Choose file” and upload your `disavow.txt`.
  6. Click “Submit”.
  7. Google will process the file. It may take a few weeks for Google to recrawl and re‑evaluate.

Important: The disavow tool does not guarantee that Google will ignore the links, but it is a strong signal. It is especially necessary when you have a manual action.

5. How to Request a Reconsideration After a Manual Action

If Google has issued a manual action against your site, you must submit a reconsideration request after disavowing.

  1. In Google Search Console, go to “Security & Manual Actions” → “Manual actions”.
  2. You will see the issue (e.g., “Unnatural links to your site”).
  3. Click “Request review”.
  4. In the text box, explain what you did to fix the issue. Example: 
    We have completed a full backlink audit. We identified 89 toxic domains that were linking to our site (mostly from purchased link schemes). We have removed all paid links and submitted a disavow file for the remaining toxic domains. We will now focus on building high‑quality, white‑hat backlinks through directories, guest posts, and HARO.
  5. Submit the request.
  6. Google typically responds within 1‑4 weeks. You will receive an email when the review is complete.

Success rate: If you have genuinely cleaned up your backlink profile and submitted a thorough reconsideration request, success rates are high (70‑80%).

6. How to Monitor After Disavowing

  • Monitor your rankings and traffic. After a successful disavow, you may see a gradual recovery over 2‑6 months.
  • In GSC, check “Manual actions” weekly to ensure no new penalties.
